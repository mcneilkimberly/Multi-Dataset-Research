Air Quality and Asthma in the United States: A State-Level Analysis

Morde Bernstein, Marley McGehee, Kimberly McNeil

5/01/2026

Introduction

Our first dataset is focused on adult asthma prevalence by state in 2020. We obtained this dataset from the CDC website at the 2020 Archived State or Territory Asthma Data page. The data has a sample size of 52, the 50 states and 2 territories of Guam and Puerto Rico, and includes both numbers with current asthma and percent with current asthma variables.

Our second dataset is about air pollution particulate matter by state, also in 2020. We obtained this data from the National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities at the Physical Environment - Air Pollution page. The sample size is 53, and the data includes air particulates in micrograms per cubic meter (PM2.5). This is a measure of air pollution which looks at the average number of particulates in the air daily.

Both of our datasets were CSVs, so we used read_csv() to open them. For the air pollution particulate matter, we skipped the first five rows because they contained metadata rather than observations. We also included show_col_types = FALSE in order to hide a non-urgent error message. Then, We standardized column names using janitor::clean_names(), then renamed the variables for clarity. Next, we turned asthma_percent’s category into a number in order to prepare it for numerical operations.

We then joined our two datasets based on states, as that was the common feature between them. After joining the datasets by state, pollution data was missing for Puerto Rico and Guam. Puerto Rico had a recorded value of NA in the pollution dataset, while Guam was not included in the pollution dataset at all. These resulted in missing values in the merged dataset. Additionally, the pollution dataset included a national-level observation (“United States”), which was excluded during merging since the asthma dataset only contains state-level observations.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Primary Key Variable Discusion

The primary key used to merge the datasets is the state (or territory) name. Both datasets include all 50 U.S. states and Washington, D.C., while the asthma dataset also includes the territories of Puerto Rico and Guam.

Some inconsistencies were present between the datasets. For example, the pollution dataset includes an observation for the United States as a whole, which does not have a corresponding entry in the asthma dataset and was thus excluded during the join. Additionally, pollution data for Puerto Rico and Guam were unavailable, leading to missing values for these locations in the merged dataset.

These issues do not significantly impact the analysis, as the focus of this project is on state-level comparisons rather than a national collection.

Research

Research Question

How does air pollution (PM2.5 levels) relate to asthma prevalence across U.S. states?

Apprach

To investigate this, we merged the asthma and pollution datasets by state and created a scatterplot to visualize the relationship. We also calculated the correlation coefficient to quantify the strength of the association, and we created a regression model to try and predict asthma prevalence using pollution. Puerto Rico and Guam were excluded from the analysis when calculating summary statistics, relationships, and the model, as pollution data for these locations was unavailable.

Results and Interpretation

Scatterplot and Correlation

The scatterplot shows a no relationship between pollution and asthma. A common way to tell if there is no relationship is if the correlation coefficient’s absolute value is between 0.0 and 0.2. Our coefficient has an absolute value of 0.066, falling within the “no relationship” interval

Because there is no relationship, we can infer that air pollution alone does not explain differences in asthma prevalence across states. Other factors (healthcare access, smoking, urbanization, environmental differences) may also play a role and may be stronger than air pollution.

Regression Model

To further examine the relationship between air pollution and asthma prevalence, we fitted a linear regression model with asthma percentage as the response variable and PM2.5 levels as the predictor.

The model found a very small negative relationship between pollution and asthma prevalence (with slope = -0.046), suggesting that higher pollution is associated with a very slight decrease in asthma rates. However, this relationship was not statistically significant (p = 0.644), indicating that there is no meaningful evidence of an association between the two variables.

Additionally, the model’s R^2 value was around 0.004, showing us that air pollution explains less than 1% of the variation in asthma prevalence across states. This tells us that that other factors (like the ones mentioned with the scatterplot) likely play a much larger role.

Again, note that two observations (Puerto Rico and Guam) were excluded from the model due to missing pollution data.

Limitations

The following is a list of some limitations of this analysis: - Our data is from one year only: 2020. - State-level averages hide local variation. - There is the absence of pollution data for Puerto Rico and Guam, which slightly reduces the completeness of the dataset.
